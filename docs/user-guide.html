1. What is the PLM Platform?

The Innopolis PLM (Product Lifecycle Management) platform is the company's single, trusted source of engineering and procurement data — the Engineering Data Backbone. Instead of materials, BOMs, vendor lists and purchase records living in scattered spreadsheets, everything lives in one connected system where each piece of data is entered once and reused everywhere.

The platform follows a project from first enquiry all the way to delivered goods, across six connected modules:

#ModuleWhat it does
1Material MasterThe catalogue of every material, with an intelligent code. The foundation for everything else.
2Project & BOMBills of Materials for each project, with a formal review-and-approval workflow.
3Vendor DatabaseYour approved suppliers and their details.
4ProcurementRequest for Quotation → compare quotes → Purchase Order → goods receipt.
5InventoryLive stock balances across warehouses, with low-stock alerts.
6Reports & AnalyticsDashboards for spend, vendor performance, stock value and project cost.
Golden rule: data flows one way and is never re-typed. A material defined once in the Master is picked into a BOM; the approved BOM feeds an RFQ; the winning quote becomes a Purchase Order; the received goods update Inventory; and every step feeds the Reports. Enter it right once, and it stays right everywhere.

2. Getting Started & Logging In

  1. Log in with the email and password given to you by your administrator.
  2. First-time login: if you were issued a temporary password, the system will require you to set a new password before you can do anything else. This is a one-time security step.
  3. Once logged in you'll see only the areas your role is allowed to use (see the next section).
If you're asked to change your password and can't continue — that's expected. The platform locks every other screen until a new password is set. Set it once and you're in.

Sessions expire after a period of inactivity for security; simply log in again if that happens. Forgot your password? Your administrator can reset it for you — they cannot see your old one.

4. The Material Code Explained

Every material carries an intelligent code — a code that tells you what the item is just by reading it. The code has four parts, each drawn from a controlled list:

TT - SS - MM - DDDD

SegmentMeansExample
TTType — the top-level category (Material Category)Fasteners
SSSubtype — a subdivision of the typeBolts
MMMajor Spec — the primary specificationM10
DDDDDetail Spec — the finer detail that makes the item unique×50mm A2

Because every segment comes from a maintained master list, codes are consistent, searchable and never duplicated. This is why the Material Master must be set up first — it defines the building blocks for every code.

5. Module 1 · Material Master

The Material Master is the catalogue at the heart of the platform. It is built in layers, from broad classification down to the individual material.

The building blocks

ItemWhat it isMaintained by
Category (Material Type)Top-level classification — the TT in the code (e.g. Fasteners, Raw Metal). Each carries a default unit of measure.Administrator
SubtypeA subdivision of a category — the SS (e.g. Bolts under Fasteners).Administrator
Major SpecPrimary specification — the MM (e.g. thread size, material grade).Administrator
GradeMaterial grade / quality reference used on parts.Administrator
UnitUnits of measure (Nos, kg, m, …) used across the system.Administrator
Part (Material)The actual catalogued material, with its intelligent code, specs, grade, unit, sourcing (Make / Buy / Standard) and lifecycle.Engineering

Category Management (worked example)

Categories are the first thing set up because they anchor the whole coding scheme. For each category the administrator records:

  • Name — e.g. "Fasteners" (must be unique).
  • Type code — the short TT code, e.g. "FS" (must be unique; stored in capitals).
  • Default unit of measure — the unit new materials in this category start with (e.g. "Nos").
  • Active / Inactive — inactive categories stay in the records but are hidden from new-material forms.
Retiring vs deleting: to stop a category from being used going forward, set it to Inactive rather than deleting it. That keeps all history intact while removing it from selection lists. Deletions are "soft" — the record is hidden, never truly erased.
Names and codes must be unique. If you try to reuse a name or type code that already exists, the system will stop you so the catalogue stays clean.

Parts — the materials themselves

A Part is a real, orderable material. Engineering creates parts by combining the building blocks above; the system assembles the intelligent code automatically. Each part also records its lifecycle stage — Concept → In Design → In Review → Released → Production → Obsolete — so everyone knows whether a material is ready to be used in production.

6. Module 2 · Project & BOM

A Project represents a customer job and moves through its own lifecycle (Enquiry → Technical Evaluation → Quotation → Project Order → Detailed Engineering → Final BOM → Purchase Release → Procurement → Fulfilment → Completed). Projects and BOMs are numbered automatically (for example INP-2026-001).

A BOM (Bill of Materials) is the structured list of every material and quantity needed to build the project's product. Engineering builds the BOM by adding lines, each pulling a material straight from the Master (so specs, unit and cost come across automatically). The platform continuously rolls up totals — total value, number of unique materials, count of critical items and long-lead items — as lines are added.

The BOM approval workflow

A BOM cannot go straight from an engineer's desk to purchasing. It passes through a controlled, auditable approval flow:

Draft→ Technical Review→ Commercial Review→ Approved→ Released for Purchase
StageWhat happensWho advances it
DraftEngineering builds and edits the BOM lines. This is the only stage where lines can be changed.Engineering
Technical ReviewThe technical content is checked.Engineering
Commercial ReviewPricing and commercial aspects are checked.Commercial
ApprovedThe BOM is signed off and ready to be released.Purchase (releases it)
Released for PurchaseLocked and handed to Procurement. This stage is final.—
  • Advancing moves the BOM to the next stage. Rejecting a review sends it back to Draft for rework.
  • A BOM with no lines cannot leave Draft, and a Released BOM can no longer be edited.
  • Every transition is recorded in an audit trail — who moved it, when, and to which stage.
BOM analysis: at any point you can analyse a BOM's cost broken down by category, vendor, lead time or make-vs-buy — useful for planning procurement and spotting long-lead or high-cost items early.

8. Module 4 · Procurement

Procurement turns an approved BOM into actual purchased goods, through a clear four-step flow:

RFQ→ Quotations→ Purchase Order→ Goods Receipt

Step 1 · Request for Quotation (RFQ)

Purchase raises an RFQ — often generated directly from a released BOM, so the items and quantities come across automatically. RFQs can be organised different ways (vendor-wise, category-wise, package-wise, single item or bulk). The RFQ starts as Draft, then is Sent to vendors.

Step 2 · Quotations & comparison

Vendors submit quotations with per-line pricing (one quotation per vendor). As quotes arrive the RFQ moves to Quotes In. The platform then compares them and ranks the offers — the cheapest gets rank 1 — so the buyer can make an objective decision. The buyer awards the winning quotation; the winner is marked Awarded and the rest Rejected automatically.

Step 3 · Purchase Order (PO)

The awarded quotation becomes a Purchase Order (POs are auto-numbered). A PO moves through its own status lifecycle:

Draft→ Pending Approval→ Open→ Partially Received→ Received→ Closed

A PO can also be Cancelled. Line totals (quantity × unit price) are calculated for you.

Step 4 · Goods Receipt

When goods arrive, Stores (or Purchase) records a receipt against the PO, entering how much was received and how much was accepted vs rejected after inspection. The PO updates to Partially Received or Received accordingly.

Rejected goods never enter stock. Only accepted quantities are posted to inventory, keeping your stock figures trustworthy. When a receiving warehouse is specified, accepted goods flow straight into Inventory automatically.

9. Module 5 · Inventory

Inventory tracks how much of each material you hold, and where. Stock is held across warehouses (Distribution, Manufacturing, Buffer or Transit) and every change is written to a permanent stock ledger — an append-only history you can always audit.

What Stores can do

  • Opening stock — record the starting quantity of a material.
  • Adjust — correct a balance (e.g. after a physical count) or record wastage.
  • Transfer — move stock from one warehouse to another.
  • Receive — accepted goods from a PO arrive as stock automatically.

What everyone can see

  • Stock balances — current quantity of every material by warehouse.
  • Movements — the full history of ins and outs.
  • Low-stock alerts — materials at or below their reorder point, so you can replenish in time.

Each material's availability is kept in step automatically — In Stock, Low Stock or Out of Stock — based on its quantity and reorder point.
